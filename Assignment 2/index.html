Nobel prize winners

Overview

Alfred Nobel was a Swedish chemist, engineer, and inventor. Before his death, he composed a will to create a series of prizes for those who confer the "greatest benefit on mankind". The Nobel prizes are awarded to the fields of Physics, Chemistry, Medicine, and Peace (The Economics prize is administered by the Nobel Foundation, but is not technically a Nobel prize). The first prize was handed out in 1901, with the reward today being a gold medal, a diploma, and 10 million Swedish krona (approx. 1.57m AUD).

Nobel prizes have been handed out to people from all over the world, such as Pakistani female education activist Malala Yousafzai, physicist Albert Einstein, and Polish physicist and chemist Marie Curie.

This data records Nobel prize winners up to 2016.

Worldly winners

The United States of America has by far the largest number of Nobel laureates, with 258, compared to the second-largest number, the United Kingdom, with 98.

Laureates are primarily dispersed through North America, Europe, East Asia, and Oceania.

Gender imbalance

The gender profile of Nobel prize laureates is extremely weighted towards males, in all prizes. However, the prize with the largest number of female recipients is the Peace prize.

The Medicine, Physics, and Chemistry prizes contain the largest number of recipients. This is likely due to the fact that in these fields, achievements for which a Nobel prize could be awarded require group efforts. In comparison, the Peace and Literature prizes are often awarded to sole recipients.

Understanding age

Nobel prize recipients are generally in their middle-age when they recieve their award, with most categories awarding prizes (usually) to people in the ages between 40 and 80.

The Economics prize is generally given out to older people, with no laureate aged under 50. Similarly, the Literature prize has not been given to a recipient under 40. Peace prize recipients are usually older, however the youngest Nobel prize recipient, Malala Yousafzai, recieved the Peace prize.

The Chemistry, Medicine, and Physics prize laureates share a similar age profile, with Physics in particular having noticeably more recipients in their 30s.

Rich and poor

The Chemistry, Economics, Medicine, and Physics prizes all share a similar makeup of GDP per capita that their laureates were born in. There is a reasonable diversity in wealth of the countries that recipients of these prizes come from, however, they are generally much more than world average GDP per capita of around $18,381USD.

On the other hand, the Literature and Peace prizes have distinctly lower interquartile ranges than the other prizes, meaning these prizes are handed to people from poorer nations more often. Examples of this can be seen with the 2011 Peace prize being given to Liberian women Ellen Johnson Sirleaf and Leymah Gbowee, and Yemeni woman Tawakkul Karman.
